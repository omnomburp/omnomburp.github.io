Animation, hierarchies, and skinning

Chapter 14

Animation works when motion flows down a hierarchy and deformation follows the rig

A model that looked fine as a static mesh becomes harder the moment it has to move. Now transforms are linked through parent-child chains, poses have to interpolate over time, and the visible surface has to follow the skeleton smoothly enough that elbows and wrists do not tear the character apart. This chapter introduces that bridge from transforms to moving characters.

Hierarchies

A child transform inherits the motion of its parent, which is why hands follow forearms and forearms follow shoulders.

Keyframes

Animation stores important poses and fills the motion between them with interpolation rules. Positions often use lerp; rotations usually use quaternion interpolation (slerp).

Skinning

Vertices can blend several bone transforms so the surface bends instead of breaking at joints.

Hierarchy global = parent * local
Slerp q(t) = q0 * (q0^-1 * q1)^t
Skinning v' = sum(w_i * M_i * v)

Game intuition

Why animation is a core system, not just character motion

Almost everything that moves in a game uses the same hierarchy and interpolation ideas. Character rigs drive walk cycles and combat moves. Camera rigs create cinematic sequences. Vehicle suspensions use parent-child chains. UI elements animate with keyframed transitions. The math behind all of these is the same transform hierarchy and interpolation system.

Character rigs

A humanoid skeleton with 50-100 joints drives walk cycles, attacks, and idles. Each joint is a child of the one above it in the body.

Facial animation

Blend shapes or bone-driven face rigs animate expressions. The jaw is a child of the head; the lips follow the jaw. Hierarchies keep the face coherent.

Vehicles and machines

Wheels are children of the chassis. Turrets are children of the hull. Doors are children of the frame. One hierarchy drives the whole assembly.

Procedural and physics-driven motion

Ragdoll physics, IK solvers, and procedural animation all feed into the same skeleton system. The output is still bone transforms that the skinning system consumes.

Mental model

Motion is a transform problem first, then a deformation problem

1

Pose the skeleton

Joint transforms establish where the major parts of the character should be right now.

2

Let children inherit parent motion

A wrist does not move in isolation. Its global transform already includes the shoulder and elbow chain above it.

3

Bind the mesh to those bones

Each vertex stores one or more bone influences so the visible surface has a rule for following the rig.

4

Blend carefully near joints

Hard ownership can produce visible seams. Weighted blending helps the surface bend more naturally.

Deep dive

Gimbal lock happens when two rotation axes collapse into the same plane

Euler angles represent a 3D rotation as three sequential rotations around fixed axes (e.g., X then Y then Z). When the middle rotation reaches 90 degrees, the first and third axes align, and the system loses one degree of freedom. Trying to rotate around the lost axis produces unexpected wobble or requires jumping through discontinuous angle values. This is gimbal lock. It is not a bug in the math; it is a fundamental limitation of representing 3D rotations as three ordered angles. Quaternions avoid it because they represent rotation as a single axis-angle pair encoded in four components, with no sequential ordering that can degenerate.

Left: Euler angles. Tilt the middle axis toward 90 degrees and watch two rings collapse onto each other, losing a rotation axis. Right: the same target rotation as a quaternion, with no axis collapse.

  1. Three sequential rotations share axis space

    Each Euler rotation happens around one axis. When the second rotation is near 90 degrees, the first and third axes become parallel.

  2. One degree of freedom disappears

    With two axes aligned, rotations around axis 1 and axis 3 produce the same motion. The system can only rotate in two independent directions.

  3. Quaternions encode rotation differently

    A quaternion stores an axis and an angle as four numbers (x, y, z, w). There is no ordering of axes that can degenerate.

When it matters most

Gimbal lock causes the worst artifacts during animation interpolation. A character's shoulder rotating near 90 degrees pitch can snap or wobble if stored as Euler angles.

Industry practice

Game engines store joint rotations as quaternions internally. Euler angles are sometimes shown in the editor for artist convenience, but the runtime always uses quaternions.

Deep dive

Slerp keeps rotation speed constant; lerp takes a shortcut through the sphere

Linear interpolation (lerp) works well for positions: it moves in a straight line at constant speed. But rotations live on the surface of a sphere. Lerp between two quaternions cuts through the interior of the sphere, producing non-uniform speed and a shorter path. Spherical linear interpolation (slerp) follows the great-circle arc on the sphere surface, maintaining constant angular velocity. The difference is most visible when interpolating large rotations or when smooth motion matters (camera paths, character joints).

Top: lerp cuts through the sphere, causing variable speed (fast in the middle, slow at endpoints). Bottom: slerp follows the arc, maintaining constant angular velocity.

Deformation story

The rig and the surface do different jobs

  1. The hierarchy solves spatial relationships

    If the shoulder rotates, the elbow and hand should already move because their frames live under the shoulder in the chain.

  2. Keyframes solve timing

    The animator stores important poses, then the system interpolates in-between values so motion feels continuous. Translations and scales are often lerped; rotations are usually blended with slerp to avoid gimbal lock and ensure constant angular speed.

  3. Skinning solves surface continuity

    The rig can move correctly while the mesh still looks bad. Skinning is the layer that turns rigid transforms into soft deformation.

  4. Secondary lag changes the feeling of weight

    Children do not always snap in perfect sync. A little delay in lower joints can make motion feel less robotic.

Common failure mode

The skeleton poses correctly, but the mesh is attached with hard ownership so the elbow collapses into obvious rigid chunks.

Quaternion interpolation

Production animation systems use slerp (spherical linear interpolation) for joint rotations. Slerp follows the shortest arc on the rotation sphere, avoiding the variable-speed artifacts that naive component-wise lerp would cause.

What "weights" mean

They are just fractions that tell each vertex how much authority each nearby bone has over its final position. Weights per vertex always sum to 1.0.

Good debugging move

Draw the bones over the mesh. If the rig motion looks right but the surface looks wrong, the bug is probably in the bind or weights.

Concept isolation

Skinning weights control how much each bone influences each vertex

Each vertex on the mesh stores a small list of bone indices and corresponding weights. When the skeleton is posed, each vertex computes its final position as a weighted blend of the bone transforms that influence it: v' = w0 * M0 * v + w1 * M1 * v + ... where M_i is the bone's current transform relative to its bind pose and w_i are the weights (summing to 1.0). The heat map below shows bone influence painted onto an arm mesh. Vertices near a joint receive significant weight from both adjacent bones, creating a smooth blend zone.

Colors represent bone influence. Pure colors mean one bone dominates. Blended colors near joints show where two bones share influence, creating smooth deformation.
