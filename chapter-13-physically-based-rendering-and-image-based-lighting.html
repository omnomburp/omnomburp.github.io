Chapter 13

A material looks believable when light, view, and environment agree on the same story

Physically based rendering is not magic realism. It is mostly discipline. Metals should reflect and tint differently from dielectrics. Rough surfaces should spread highlights instead of keeping them razor sharp. The surrounding world should matter because real objects do not only respond to one hand-placed light. Image-based lighting is one way to give that surrounding world a concrete role.

Metalness

Controls whether the surface behaves more like a colored conductor or a dielectric with a small neutral F0.

Roughness

Widens and softens the specular response instead of acting like a vague "shininess" number.

Environment light

Lets reflections and ambient response depend on the world around the object instead of only on one direct light.

Game intuition

Why PBR became the default for real-time materials

Before PBR, artists tuned separate diffuse color, specular color, shininess, and ambient values per material per scene. Moving an asset to a different lighting setup often meant re-tweaking everything. PBR constrains the material response to physical rules, which means a material authored once looks plausible under many different lighting conditions.

Weapons and armor

Metal armor reflects the environment and tints reflections with its base color. Leather straps stay diffuse. One material model handles both on the same character.

Wet and dry surfaces

Rain on a cobblestone road lowers roughness and raises F0 at the surface. PBR makes this a parameter change, not a separate shader.

Environment transitions

A character walks from a dark cave into sunlight. PBR materials respond naturally because the environment cubemap changes, not the material knobs.

Asset reuse across scenes

A prop authored for one level can be placed in another without re-tweaking. The material describes physical properties, not scene-specific hacks.

Mental model

Modern material response is structured, not arbitrary

1

Diffuse and specular are not independent decorations

They are two ways light leaves a surface, and good material models keep them in a more coherent relationship.

2

Metals tint reflections strongly

Dielectrics keep a small neutral base reflectance. Metals push more of the base color into the reflective response.

3

Roughness changes shape, not just brightness

A rough highlight should broaden and blur. It is not enough to merely turn the highlight down.

4

Environment lighting stabilizes the look

As the camera moves, the material still feels grounded because the surrounding world keeps contributing light and reflections.

Deep dive

The Fresnel effect makes edges more reflective than head-on views

When you look at a lake, the water near the horizon acts like a mirror while the water at your feet shows the bottom. This is the Fresnel effect: reflectance increases at grazing angles. Schlick's approximation models it cheaply: F = F0 + (1 - F0) * (1 - cosθ)^5, where F0 is the reflectance at normal incidence and θ is the angle between the view direction and the surface normal. For dielectrics, F0 is small (around 0.04). For metals, F0 is the base color itself. The exponent of 5 produces a rapid ramp near grazing angles.

The sphere shows reflectance increasing from center (normal incidence, low F) to rim (grazing angle, high F). The curve on the right plots Schlick's approximation for the current F0.

Deep dive

The specular lobe is the angular distribution of reflected energy

A perfectly smooth mirror reflects all incoming light in exactly one direction. A rough surface scatters that light into a cone. The specular lobe describes the shape of that cone: how wide or narrow the reflected energy spreads around the perfect mirror direction. Low roughness produces a tight, bright lobe (sharp highlight). High roughness produces a wide, dim lobe (soft, spread-out highlight). The total energy in the lobe stays roughly the same; it just gets redistributed over a larger area.

The lobe shape changes with roughness. Low roughness concentrates reflected energy into a tight cone; high roughness spreads it wide. The total reflected energy is roughly constant.

Material story

PBR is mostly about using fewer arbitrary cheats

  1. Start with a base color that means something

    For dielectrics it mostly describes diffuse appearance. For metals it also drives the reflected tint more strongly.

  2. Let Fresnel increase reflection toward grazing angles

    Even simple models benefit when edges feel more reflective than head-on views. Schlick's approximation: F = F0 + (1 - F0)(1 - cosθ)^5.

  3. Use roughness to reshape the highlight and reflection

    Low roughness keeps a concentrated lobe. High roughness spreads that energy over a wider region.

  4. Light the object with a world, not just a lamp

    Image-based lighting gives the material something believable to reflect and picks up broad ambient cues from the scene. A cubemap stores the environment as six face textures; a direction vector maps to a face and UV coordinate.

Why this helps artists

Material controls become more transferable across scenes because the knobs map to more stable physical ideas.

Common failure mode

A surface has bright specular response and strong diffuse color at the same time in a way that feels energy-incoherent.

IBL and cubemaps

A cubemap is a six-face box texture. The renderer converts a reflection direction into a face index and UV coordinate to sample the environment. Pre-filtered mip levels store blurred versions for rougher surfaces.

Good debugging move

Turn roughness to the extremes and rotate the environment. If nothing qualitative changes, the shader model is probably too weak.

Concept isolation

Metals and dielectrics split the same base color into different channels

A dielectric (plastic, wood, stone) has a small neutral F0 around 0.04 and puts most of its base color into diffuse response. A metal has no diffuse response; instead, its base color becomes the F0, tinting reflections with the material color. The metalness slider interpolates between these two behaviors. This is why a gold sphere reflects gold-tinted highlights while a yellow plastic sphere has white highlights.

Left: dielectric sphere with white specular highlights and colored diffuse. Right: metal sphere with tinted specular reflections and no diffuse. Same base color, different material class.

Deep dive

Energy conservation keeps materials from emitting more light than they receive

A physically plausible surface must not reflect more energy than arrives. If a large fraction goes into specular reflection, less is available for diffuse. PBR enforces this as a constraint: diffuse + specular <= 1.0 at every point. Without energy conservation, a surface can look unnaturally bright or glowing, especially at grazing angles where Fresnel boosts specular contribution. The bar chart below shows how the diffuse and specular fractions change with metalness and roughness, always staying within the energy budget.

The diffuse and specular bars always sum to at most 1.0. As metalness increases, diffuse shrinks to zero and specular takes over. As Fresnel ramps at the edge, specular grows while diffuse is reduced accordingly.
