Cameras and projection

Chapter 6

A camera is a viewpoint plus a projection rule

The camera does two jobs. First, it defines where the eye is and what direction it faces. Second, it decides how 3D positions should be mapped onto a 2D image. Perspective projection makes distant objects appear smaller. Orthographic projection keeps parallel size relationships intact.

Field of view

Controls how wide the camera lens feels and how much of the scene can fit into the frame.

Perspective

Projection rays meet at the eye, so the same-sized object appears smaller as it moves farther away.

Orthographic

Projection rays stay parallel, so size stays constant with distance. The result feels more measured than photographic.

Perspective hint x_screen ~ x / z
Narrow field of view smaller angle -> zoomed in
Orthographic rule parallel lines stay parallel

Mental model

Projection is not geometry. It is the camera’s rule for making 3D readable on a flat screen.

1

The camera first defines view space

Before projection happens, the scene is re-expressed relative to the eye so “forward” and “distance” are camera-relative ideas.

2

Perspective compresses with depth

Farther objects contribute smaller image-space values after the perspective divide, which is why the scene feels spatial.

3

Orthographic ignores distance scaling

It still projects the scene, but it does not shrink distant geometry, so the image feels more diagrammatic than photographic.

4

Field of view changes framing, not object size in world space

A wider field of view shows more of the scene, but it also exaggerates perspective near the camera.

Camera Shapes

Perspective uses a frustum. Orthographic uses a box-like volume.

These are not just different formulas. They are different camera models. Perspective has a single eye point and converging rays. Orthographic keeps the rays parallel and the view volume box-shaped.

Visual explainer

Same three objects, same scene order. Perspective shrinks with depth; orthographic keeps their screen heights aligned.

  1. Perspective starts from an eye point

    All visible points are projected toward the image through one viewpoint, which is why distant objects shrink.

  2. The visible volume is a frustum

    It is wide near the camera direction and expands with depth according to the chosen field of view.

  3. Orthographic keeps projection rays parallel

    The visible volume is more like a box, so objects do not get smaller with distance.

  4. Camera distance and field of view are not the same control

    Moving the camera changes placement. Changing field of view changes the lens. Those can feel similar in framing, but they are not the same visual cause.

Perspective feeling

Spatial, lens-like, cinematic, and more sensitive to near-camera distortion when the field of view is wide.

Orthographic feeling

Measured, diagrammatic, and useful when preserving parallel relationships matters more than depth realism.

Step by step

What the camera and projection matrices are doing

  1. Choose the eye position

    The camera has to know where it is and what it is looking at before any image can be formed.

  2. Build the view transform

    This rewrites the world relative to the camera so the eye behaves like the origin of the visible scene.

  3. Pick a projection model

    Perspective gives a human-camera feel. Orthographic gives measurement-like consistency.

  4. Map into clip space and divide

    After projection, you have a clip-space coordinate. Dividing by w produces normalized device coordinates, which the viewport then maps to pixels.

Good beginner distinction

Camera motion changes the frame of reference. Projection changes how depth turns into screen size.

Clip space is not yet the screen

The projection matrix writes a homogeneous clip coordinate. The perspective divide creates NDC, and only the viewport step turns NDC into pixel positions.

Common bug

If the near plane is too close or the far plane too large, depth precision can get worse than you expect.

Why this chapter matters

Once cameras make sense, texture projection, shadow maps, and screen-space effects become easier to organize.

Depth precision

A near plane that is too close can waste depth precision and make distant surfaces fight

Beginners often treat near and far planes as harmless clipping choices. They are also part of the depth precision story. Perspective depth values are distributed unevenly, so pushing the near plane too close to zero can make distant surfaces numerically harder to separate.

Visual explainer

A tighter near plane preserves more usable separation for distant depth values. An excessively tiny near plane makes far surfaces crowd together.

  1. Depth values are not spread evenly in perspective projection

    Much more precision is spent near the camera than far away, which is why near-plane choices matter so much.

  2. Bringing the near plane closer changes that distribution

    The farther range gets squeezed more tightly, so two distant surfaces can land on nearly identical stored depths.

  3. That leads to z-fighting

    If two surfaces are close together and the stored depth cannot separate them well, the image may flicker between them.

  4. The fix is usually to tighten the range, especially the near plane

    Good clipping values are not only about visibility. They are also about preserving stable depth comparisons.

Good rule of thumb

Move the near plane out as far as the scene allows before you reach for more exotic fixes.

What to watch for

If distant coplanar or almost-coplanar surfaces shimmer, the problem may be depth precision long before it is a shader bug.

Live lab

Switch between projection models and change the lens

The scene contains several cubes at different depths. Narrow the field of view and the view feels zoomed in. Turn on orthographic mode and the depth-based size change disappears, even though the 3D arrangement is the same.

  • First leave orthographic mode off and vary only the field of view. Watch how wide-angle perspective stretches space.
  • Then turn orthographic mode on and compare the same geometry without depth-based shrinking.
  • Finally move the camera farther back and ask which visual changes come from camera placement and which from projection type.

Projection is the rule that turns 3D depth into a 2D image, not a change to the scene itself.
