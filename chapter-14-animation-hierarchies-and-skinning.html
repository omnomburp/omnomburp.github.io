Animation, hierarchies, and skinning

Chapter 14

Animation works when motion flows down a hierarchy and deformation follows the rig

A model that looked fine as a static mesh becomes harder the moment it has to move. Now transforms are linked through parent-child chains, poses have to interpolate over time, and the visible surface has to follow the skeleton smoothly enough that elbows and wrists do not tear the character apart. This chapter introduces that bridge from transforms to moving characters.

Hierarchies

A child transform inherits the motion of its parent, which is why hands follow forearms and forearms follow shoulders.

Keyframes

Animation stores important poses and fills the motion between them with interpolation rules. Positions often use lerp; rotations usually use quaternion interpolation.

Skinning

Vertices can blend several bone transforms so the surface bends instead of breaking at joints.

Hierarchy global = parent * local
One position blend position = lerp(A, B, t)
Skinning idea vertex = sum(weight_i * bone_i(vertex))

Mental model

Motion is a transform problem first, then a deformation problem

1

Pose the skeleton

Joint transforms establish where the major parts of the character should be right now.

2

Let children inherit parent motion

A wrist does not move in isolation. Its global transform already includes the shoulder and elbow chain above it.

3

Bind the mesh to those bones

Each vertex stores one or more bone influences so the visible surface has a rule for following the rig.

4

Blend carefully near joints

Hard ownership can produce visible seams. Weighted blending helps the surface bend more naturally.

Deformation story

The rig and the surface do different jobs

  1. The hierarchy solves spatial relationships

    If the shoulder rotates, the elbow and hand should already move because their frames live under the shoulder in the chain.

  2. Keyframes solve timing

    The animator stores important poses, then the system interpolates in-between values so motion feels continuous. Translations and scales are often lerped; rotations are usually blended with quaternion interpolation.

  3. Skinning solves surface continuity

    The rig can move correctly while the mesh still looks bad. Skinning is the layer that turns rigid transforms into soft deformation.

  4. Secondary lag changes the feeling of weight

    Children do not always snap in perfect sync. A little delay in lower joints can make motion feel less robotic.

Common failure mode

The skeleton poses correctly, but the mesh is attached with hard ownership so the elbow collapses into obvious rigid chunks.

Where quaternions enter later

This chapter stays conceptual. Production systems often interpolate rotations with quaternions to avoid the artifacts that simple component-wise Euler blending can cause in 3D joints.

What “weights” mean

They are just fractions that tell each vertex how much authority each nearby bone has over its final position.

Good debugging move

Draw the bones over the mesh. If the rig motion looks right but the surface looks wrong, the bug is probably in the bind or weights.

Live lab

Pose a simple arm rig and compare rigid attachment against smooth skinning

The skeleton below drives a simple arm-like strip. Use the pose slider to bend the chain, add lag so child joints trail slightly, then switch between rigid ownership and smooth skinning. The rig motion stays similar in both modes. What changes is how the surface interprets it near the joints.

  • Turn smooth skinning off first. The arm should bend with sharper seams where ownership changes from one bone to the next.
  • Turn smooth skinning on. The same pose should deform more continuously around the elbow and wrist.
  • Increase child lag and notice how the hand trails the shoulder slightly, which changes the feeling of weight and timing.

The white line is the arm mesh. The joint chain underneath it is the rig. The lesson is that hierarchy and skinning solve different problems, even when they drive the same motion.

Self-test

Common mistake

Thinking the skeleton and the mesh are the same data. The rig drives motion; the mesh is the visible surface that interprets it.
