Chapter 3

Surface normals explain why light changes across a shape

Shading gets much easier once you stop thinking in terms of “this triangle should be bright” and start thinking in terms of “this surface is facing the light.” Surface normals provide that facing direction.

Core skill

Read a normal as an orientation vector attached to the surface.

Lighting link

Use the dot product between a normal and a light direction to measure alignment.

Debugging habit

If lighting looks wrong, inspect normals before blaming the material.

Diffuse light brightness = max(dot(n, l), 0)
Perfect alignment dot(n, l) = 1
Facing away dot(n, l) <= 0

Game Intuition

A flashlight sliding across armor is a normal-direction story

In a game, a shield, helmet, wall, or character shoulder can stay in the same place while its brightness changes dramatically. The missing ingredient is usually the surface normal, not a mystery light rule.

A moving flashlight hits a curved shield. The highlighted point changes brightness because the local normal and incoming light direction align differently over time.

Surface normal

Light direction

The orange arrow points from the surface point toward the flashlight.

l

Alignment test

The brighter patch is the one whose normal lines up best with the light.

dot(n, l)

Game effect

This is why a curved surface gets a moving highlight instead of one flat brightness value.

diffuse = max(dot(n, l), 0)

  1. The flashlight is not asking “where is the shield?”

    It already knows where the surface point is. The shading question is which way that point is facing.

  2. Different points on the same object can face the light differently

    That is why a round shield or shoulder pad gets a bright hotspot that slides instead of one uniform color.

  3. The normal changes the answer even when the light stays fixed

    Move to a different patch on the curved surface and the angle to the flashlight changes, so the diffuse term changes too.

  4. This same rule drives many game materials

    Characters, props, terrain, and walls all start from this same normal-versus-light alignment test before more advanced shading layers are added.

Useful debug habit

If a highlight appears in the wrong place on a model, check the normals before tweaking light intensity or material values.

Why chapter 3 matters

Once this picture is clear, shader code stops looking like mysterious dot-product folklore and starts reading like visible geometry logic.

Mental model

A normal is not where the surface is. It is how the surface is oriented.

1

Normals are directions

They point away from the surface and tell you which way that part of the shape is facing. Many mesh normals begin as cross(edge1, edge2).

2

Light has a direction too

To decide how much light arrives, compare the light direction to the surface normal.

3

The dot product measures alignment

If two vectors point together, the value is large. If they point apart, the value drops.

4

Interpolated normals smooth the look

Normals can vary across a triangle, which makes a faceted mesh appear smoothly shaded.

Reinforcement

Reuse normals in several concrete graphics decisions

A normal is more than “the arrow lighting uses.” The same direction data keeps showing up in diffuse response, facing tests, face construction, and shading style. Seeing those together makes the chapter much harder to forget.

Diffuse lighting is just alignment

When the light arrow and the normal arrow line up, the patch gets brighter. That is the same dot(n, l) you will see in shader code.

diffuse = max(dot(n, l), 0)

Facing the camera is another dot test

The surface can be front-facing or back-facing relative to the view direction long before any material shading happens.

dot(n, viewDir)

Triangle normals come from edges

A mesh face does not magically know its orientation. Two edge vectors can build the perpendicular direction explicitly.

n = normalize(cross(e1, e2))

Shading style depends on which normal you feed forward

One face normal gives a faceted look. Interpolated vertex normals make the same coarse mesh read as smoothly shaded.

vNormal -> fragment

Step by step

How the lighting term is built

  1. Normalize the normal

    Use a unit-length direction so the dot product reports alignment instead of getting mixed up with length.

  2. Normalize the light direction

    Now both vectors talk about orientation in the same way.

  3. Take the dot product

    This gives a value from about -1 to 1, depending on how aligned the vectors are.

  4. Clamp negative values

    Surfaces facing away from the light should not receive positive diffuse light, so values below zero are clipped.

Non-uniform scale warning

Normals are directions, not positions. Under non-uniform scale, they need a special transform called the inverse transpose.

Why visualizing helps

When you draw the normals as lines, lighting bugs often become obvious immediately.

What this unlocks next

    Normal maps

    A texture can store perturbed normals so a flat surface looks detailed

    Normal mapping encodes small surface bumps as direction vectors packed into RGB texels. The fragment shader reads the per-texel normal instead of the interpolated vertex normal, adding visual detail without extra geometry. A flat quad can look like carved stone.

    Left: vertex normals only — the surface is smooth and featureless. Right: the same geometry with a procedural normal map — light reveals bumps that do not exist in the mesh.

    The geometry stays flat

    Normal maps add visual complexity without adding triangles. The silhouette remains unchanged, but the interior lighting creates an illusion of surface detail.

    RGB stores XYZ

    Each texel's red, green, and blue channels encode a direction vector. The value (128, 128, 255) maps to (0, 0, 1) — pointing straight out of the surface.

    The dominant color is blue-purple

    Most normals point roughly outward, so the Z component is close to 1.0 and the blue channel dominates. Slight tilts shift the red and green channels away from 128.

    Lighting uses the texture normal

    The fragment shader substitutes the sampled normal for the interpolated vertex normal before computing the dot product with the light direction.

    // sample and unpack
vec3 nTex = texture(normalMap, uv).rgb;
vec3 n = nTex * 2.0 - 1.0;
// n is now in tangent space: (0,0,1) = flat

// lighting uses this instead of vertex normal
float diffuse = max(dot(n, lightDir), 0.0);

    Why 2.0 - 1.0?

    Texture channels store values in [0, 1]. Normals live in [-1, 1]. The * 2 - 1 remapping converts storage range to direction range.

    Limitation: silhouettes

    Normal maps change shading but not geometry. At grazing angles, the flat silhouette reveals that the bumps are an illusion. Displacement mapping solves this by actually moving vertices.

    Tangent space

    Normal maps are authored in a local frame that needs a TBN matrix to reach world space

    A normal map stores directions relative to a flat surface pointing up. When that surface is tilted, rotated, or curved in the scene, you need a per-vertex coordinate frame — the tangent, bitangent, and normal — to transform the map's local directions into world space. That 3×3 matrix is called the TBN matrix.

    A tilted surface patch with its tangent (red), bitangent (green), and normal (blue) axes. The TBN matrix columns are these three vectors. Multiplying a tangent-space normal by TBN produces the world-space normal.

    T: tangent

    The tangent vector lies along the surface in the direction of increasing U in the texture. It comes from the triangle's edge vectors and UV differences.

    B: bitangent

    The bitangent lies along the surface in the V direction, perpendicular to both the tangent and the normal. It is often computed as cross(N, T).

    N: normal

    The vertex normal you already know. It points away from the surface and is the third axis of the local frame.

    TBN transforms tangent-space to world-space

    worldNormal = TBN * tangentNormal. The columns of TBN are T, B, and N. A tangent-space (0, 0, 1) maps directly to the vertex normal — no perturbation.

    // build TBN per vertex
mat3 TBN = mat3(T, B, N);

// transform sampled normal to world space
vec3 n = TBN * (texture(normalMap, uv).rgb * 2.0 - 1.0);
n = normalize(n);

// then use n for lighting as usual
float diffuse = max(dot(n, lightDir), 0.0);

    Where tangents come from

    Tangents are derived from the triangle's edge vectors and UV coordinate differences. Most mesh exporters precompute them, or you can compute them at load time from the position and UV attributes.

    Orthogonalization

    Interpolation can make T and N non-perpendicular. A Gram-Schmidt step re-orthogonalizes them in the fragment shader: T = normalize(T - dot(T, N) * N).

    TBN lab

    Tilt the surface and watch the TBN frame follow the geometry

    The surface patch tilts in 3D. The tangent, bitangent, and normal axes rotate with it. When the normal map is enabled, each fragment reads a perturbed direction from the map, transforms it through TBN, and lights the surface with the result. Toggle the map off to see how flat the surface actually is.

    • Tilt the surface to 0 degrees. The TBN frame aligns with the world axes.
    • Toggle the normal map off. The surface goes flat — all detail disappears.
    • Move the light angle with the map on. The bumps respond to light direction just like real geometry would.
    • At extreme tilts, the TBN frame rotates with the surface, keeping bump lighting correct.
