Color, tone mapping, and post-processing

This chapter explains why a physically sensible render can still look wrong if the image is displayed or processed incorrectly.

Chapter 11

Rendering does not end when the shader writes a bright value

The GPU may compute values that are brighter than a display can show directly. Tone mapping compresses those values into a viewable range. A final display-encoding step, often approximated with gamma correction in beginner examples, makes sure the final values are prepared for how real displays respond. Post-processing then adds finishing passes like bloom, color grading, or vignette.

Linear color

This is where most lighting math should happen so light adds and scales correctly.

Tone mapping

Compresses HDR-like values into the limited range the display can show.

Display encoding

Often taught as a gamma step, but in practice usually handled with an sRGB or other display transfer function.

Exposure color *= exposure
Simple tone map mapped = color / (1 + color)
Display encode (approx.) display = pow(mapped, 1 / gamma)

Mental model

The rendered image is raw light data until it is prepared for a display device

1

Lighting math belongs in linear space

Adding and scaling light only behaves correctly when the numbers reflect actual proportional intensity.

2

HDR values often exceed display range

Bright highlights can produce values above 1.0, which means they need range compression before display.

3

Tone mapping reshapes contrast

It preserves the feeling of brightness differences while squeezing them into a smaller output range.

4

Display encoding is the final display handshake

Without it, the image can look too dark or perceptually wrong even if the scene math was fine.

Display story

The same light values look different in a linear buffer, after tone mapping, and after display encoding

These terms are easy to blur together because they all happen near the end of the pipeline. They are not the same operation. One is about physically meaningful working values, one is about range compression, and one is about matching the display.

Visual explainer

The samples are the same light story in every panel. What changes is how they are prepared for a limited display.

  1. The linear working image may contain values above 1.0

    That is normal when a highlight is much brighter than the surrounding image. The renderer is still describing light, not yet a monitor-ready picture.

  2. Tone mapping compresses that wide range into something viewable

    It keeps bright areas feeling brighter while preventing them from clipping as harshly as a raw display clamp would.

  3. Display encoding reshapes the final values for the screen

    In this course that is shown as a simple gamma-style step, but real pipelines often target a proper sRGB transfer instead of a pure power curve.

  4. Post-processing usually belongs before the final display handoff

    Bloom, grading, and similar passes are easier to reason about while the image is still in a controlled working space.

Good mental split

Linear is for light math. Tone mapping is for fitting range. Display encoding is for the monitor.

Why images look washed out

A bug in any one of those three stages can make the final result look wrong even if the geometry and shading are both correct.

Visual explainer

Tone mapping compresses HDR values into the displayable range

Linear rendering can produce values far above 1.0 — a sunlit surface might be 4 or 5 times brighter than a shaded wall. Tone mapping applies a curve that compresses those high values while preserving contrast in the mid-range. Different operators (Reinhard, ACES) shape that compression differently.

The graph shows input HDR values on the x-axis and mapped display values on the y-axis. The dashed white line is the identity (no compression). Toggle Reinhard and ACES curves to compare how each operator handles highlights. The exposure slider scales all input values.

  • With default exposure, Reinhard gently compresses highlights toward 1.0 but never quite reaches it.
  • Toggle ACES on to see the filmic S-curve: darker shadows, punchier midtones, and a more aggressive highlight rolloff.
  • Push exposure above 2× and watch both curves compress harder to keep everything displayable.

Reinhard (teal) and ACES (orange) shape the HDR-to-display mapping differently. Sample dots show where typical scene values land on each curve.

Step by step

How a rendered frame becomes a displayed image

  1. Shade into a linear working image

    This is where physically meaningful light values should accumulate.

  2. Apply exposure and tone mapping

    Bright values are compressed into a viewable range while preserving relative contrast.

  3. Run post-processing effects

    Bloom, color grading, vignette, film grain, and sharpening are often layered after the main shading pass.

  4. Encode for display

    The image is finally transformed into a display-friendly space, often with gamma encoding or an sRGB transfer.

Why bloom exists

It helps very bright highlights feel intense even after tone mapping compresses the numerical range.

Simple gamma is not the whole story

This chapter uses a power curve because it is easy to see. Production pipelines often target an sRGB transfer or another display-specific encoding rather than a pure pow step.

Why this chapter is last

You need the earlier pipeline in your head before it becomes clear that final image presentation is its own discipline.

Good debugging move

When an image looks washed out or too dark, separate the problem into lighting, tone mapping, and display encoding rather than guessing blindly.
