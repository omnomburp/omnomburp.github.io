Chapter 1

Vectors, bases, and matrix transforms

Computer graphics begins with a simple question: how do we describe points and directions in space? Vectors answer that question. Bases tell us what the coordinate axes mean. Matrices tell us how to rotate, scale, shear, or otherwise reinterpret those coordinates.

What you should leave with

Confidence reading a point as weighted basis directions rather than a mysterious pair of numbers.

Main idea

A matrix changes how a shape is interpreted inside a coordinate frame.

Why it matters

Everything later in graphics depends on moving data between spaces without losing track of meaning.

Coordinates as weights p = x i + y j
Transform a point p' = M p
Order matters B(Ap) != A(Bp)

Mental model

Think of coordinates as instructions for rebuilding a point from basis directions

The numbers inside a vector are not meaningful by themselves. They only become meaningful after you decide what the basis directions are. In graphics, changing the basis is often just as important as changing the point.

1

A vector is direction plus magnitude

Picture an arrow. Its orientation tells you where it points, and its length tells you how much movement it represents.

2

A basis picks the local language

The usual 2D basis is “one step right” and “one step up,” but you are free to rotate or stretch that language.

3

Coordinates are basis weights

If a point is (2, 1), read that as “two copies of the x basis plus one copy of the y basis.”

4

A matrix changes the frame

Rotation, scaling, and shearing can all be seen as changing the basis vectors used to rebuild the point.

Step by step

Walk one point through a transform chain

  1. Start with a local point

    Imagine the point (1, 0.5) inside a triangle. In its original basis, that means one unit along x and half a unit along y.

  2. Scale the basis

    If the x basis doubles in length, every x coordinate effectively covers more space. The shape widens without rewriting every vertex by hand.

  3. Rotate the basis

    Now turn those basis directions. The same coordinate weights now rebuild the point in a new orientation.

  4. Compose transforms carefully

    Scaling and then rotating is not the same as rotating and then scaling, because the second transform acts on the output of the first.

Say it in plain language

“This matrix changes the axes, so the same shape coordinates now produce a different picture.”

Beginner trap

Do not think of matrices as arbitrary tables of numbers. Treat them as compact descriptions of geometric behavior.

What this unlocks next

Once transforms feel natural, moving from object space to world space becomes much less intimidating.

Live lab

Use the controls to see the basis change in real time

The pale triangle is the original object. The bright triangle is the transformed version. The axes attached to the transformed object show the new basis directions after the matrix is applied.

  • Set both stretch sliders to 100 and rotate the object. That isolates pure rotation.
  • Set rotation to 0 and change one stretch slider at a time. That isolates basis length changes.
  • Combine both and notice how the local axes move with the transformed shape, not with the world grid.

The world grid stays fixed while the transform matrix changes the object’s local frame.

Checkpoints

Questions you should be able to answer before moving on

Self-test

Can you explain what a point like (3, 2) means without saying “just x and y”?

Common mistake

Forgetting that transform order matters. A later matrix acts on the already-transformed result.

Vocabulary to keep

Vector, basis, coordinate frame, transform, scale, rotation, shear, composition.
