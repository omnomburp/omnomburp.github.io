Color, tone mapping, and post-processing

This final chapter explains why a physically sensible render can still look wrong if the image is displayed or processed incorrectly.

Chapter 11

Rendering does not end when the shader writes a bright value

The GPU may compute values that are brighter than a display can show directly. Tone mapping compresses those values into a viewable range. Gamma correction makes sure the final values are encoded for how displays actually respond. Post-processing then adds finishing passes like bloom, color grading, or vignette.

Linear color

This is where most lighting math should happen so light adds and scales correctly.

Tone mapping

Compresses HDR-like values into the limited range the display can show.

Gamma correction

Transforms the final image so the displayed brightness feels visually correct on the screen.

Exposure color *= exposure
Simple tone map mapped = color / (1 + color)
Gamma encode display = pow(mapped, 1 / gamma)

Mental model

The rendered image is raw light data until it is prepared for a display device

1

Lighting math belongs in linear space

Adding and scaling light only behaves correctly when the numbers reflect actual proportional intensity.

2

HDR values often exceed display range

Bright highlights can produce values above 1.0, which means they need range compression before display.

3

Tone mapping reshapes contrast

It preserves the feeling of brightness differences while squeezing them into a smaller output range.

4

Gamma correction is the final display handshake

Without it, the image can look too dark or perceptually wrong even if the scene math was fine.

Step by step

How a rendered frame becomes a displayed image

  1. Shade into a linear working image

    This is where physically meaningful light values should accumulate.

  2. Apply exposure and tone mapping

    Bright values are compressed into a viewable range while preserving relative contrast.

  3. Run post-processing effects

    Bloom, color grading, vignette, film grain, and sharpening are often layered after the main shading pass.

  4. Encode for display

    The image is finally transformed into a display-friendly space, often with gamma encoding or an sRGB transfer.

Why bloom exists

It helps very bright highlights feel intense even after tone mapping compresses the numerical range.

Why this chapter is last

You need the earlier pipeline in your head before it becomes clear that final image presentation is its own discipline.

Good debugging move

When an image looks washed out or too dark, separate the problem into lighting, tone mapping, and display encoding rather than guessing blindly.

Live lab

Adjust exposure and gamma on an HDR-like procedural image

The shader below generates bright highlights and a glowing sky in linear-like space. Use the sliders to see how exposure, tone mapping, and gamma each change the final displayed image. This is not about geometry anymore. It is about image preparation.

  • Turn tone mapping off and raise exposure. The highlights clip aggressively because the display range is exceeded.
  • Turn tone mapping on and compare how the brightest areas compress more gracefully.
  • Change gamma and notice that midtones shift perceptually even when the underlying scene structure stays the same.

The final displayed image depends on exposure, tone mapping, and display encoding, not just on shading.
