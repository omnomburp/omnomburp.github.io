Chapter 8

A material is a rule for how a surface responds to light and to the viewer

Diffuse light explains broad brightness changes, but it does not explain shiny highlights, glints, or why some surfaces look matte and others look polished. A lighting model combines multiple terms so the surface can respond to both the light direction and the eye direction.

Diffuse response

This is the broad light term controlled mainly by normal-light alignment.

Specular response

This creates concentrated highlights that depend strongly on view direction.

Material feel

Changing roughness, shininess, and balance between terms changes whether a surface feels soft, glossy, or sharp.

Mental model

Lighting models split the look into parts so each visual behavior has a reason

1

Diffuse handles broad illumination

This is the part that tracks whether the surface faces toward the light source.

2

Specular handles glossy reflection

The highlight moves as the camera moves because it depends on both light direction and view direction.

3

Ambient or fill terms keep shadows readable

Real scenes often have bounced light, so pure black shadows are usually too simple for a convincing result.

4

Material parameters shape the feel

A broader highlight can feel rougher. A tighter one can feel more polished or harder.

Response story

Diffuse and specular are different answers to different geometric questions

At one surface point, the shader is really asking two related questions: how much the light faces the surface, and whether the viewer is near the direction of a glossy reflection. Those two answers produce very different looks.

Visual explainer

The same surface point keeps one normal, but changing the light and view directions changes diffuse fill and glossy response for different reasons.

  1. Diffuse depends on the normal and the light direction

    When the light grazes the surface, the diffuse term falls off. When the light faces the surface more directly, the diffuse term grows.

  2. Specular depends on the viewer as well

    A glossy highlight only becomes strong when the eye lines up with a favored reflection direction.

  3. The half-vector or reflection alignment controls highlight intensity

    That is why a highlight can slide across the surface as the camera moves even when the light does not.

  4. Shininess changes the width of the highlight

    A low shininess value spreads the energy into a broad patch. A high value concentrates it into a tight glossy spot.

Step by step

Build a beginner-friendly lighting model in layers

  1. Start with a base color

    The material usually has a diffuse albedo or base color that describes its broad tint under light.

  2. Add diffuse light

    Use the normal-light alignment to decide how strongly the base color should respond.

  3. Add a specular highlight

    Now compute a view-dependent term that creates the glossy concentrated bright spot.

  4. Tune the balance

    Changing specular strength and shininess does not move the geometry. It changes the story the light tells about the surface.

Blinn-Phong intuition

Many beginner renderers use Blinn-Phong because it is simple, stable, and good enough to teach diffuse plus specular logic.

Real materials are richer

Physically based shading adds more accurate energy behavior, roughness handling, and material categories, but the same intuition still helps.

Do the lighting math in linear color

If a base-color texture was authored in sRGB, decode it before combining it with diffuse or specular terms. Normal and roughness maps are already data, not display colors.

The sphere below keeps the same shape while you change only its lighting response. Increase the specular strength to create a brighter highlight. Increase shininess to tighten that highlight into a smaller glossier patch.

  • Lower specular strength to zero and observe that the highlight disappears even though diffuse shading remains.
  • Raise shininess and watch the bright region tighten into a smaller sharper reflection.

The geometry does not change. The apparent material changes because the light-response rule changes.
