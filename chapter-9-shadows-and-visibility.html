Chapter 9

A shadow appears wherever the light cannot see the surface

Once lighting models are in place, the next natural question is why some parts of the scene receive no direct light. The answer is visibility. If an object blocks the path from the light to the surface, that surface point falls into shadow.

Blockers

Objects cast shadows because they stop light from reaching other parts of the scene.

Shadow maps

A common real-time trick is to render the scene from the light’s view first and reuse that depth later.

Artifacts

Shadows are prone to bias issues, resolution problems, and self-shadowing errors.

Shadow test idea is this point farther than light depth?
If yes blocked -> in shadow
If no visible to light

Mental model

Shadows are geometry plus visibility, not a separate decorative effect

1

Light behaves like another camera

To ask whether a point is shadowed, you can imagine the scene from the light’s point of view.

2

The closest surface to the light wins

If a surface point lies farther away than the light’s stored depth at that pixel, something else blocked it first.

3

Resolution limits matter

A low-resolution shadow map has to approximate many world-space details with too few samples.

4

Bias is a balancing act

Too little bias causes self-shadowing acne. Too much bias makes the shadow detach from the caster.

Step by step

How a basic shadow map pipeline works

  1. Render depth from the light’s view

    This creates a texture that stores the closest visible surface distance for each light-space pixel.

  2. Render the scene normally from the camera

    For each visible fragment, transform its world position into the light’s projection space.

  3. Look up the stored light depth

    Sample the shadow map at the fragment’s light-space coordinate.

  4. Compare depths

    If the fragment is farther than what the light saw first, that fragment is shadowed by a blocker.

Why this works

The light’s depth map is a visibility memory: it remembers which surface got there first from the light’s perspective.

Soft shadows

Filtering multiple nearby shadow-map samples can fake penumbra softness, though real soft shadows are more complicated.

Why it is hard

You are combining two different camera systems, two projections, and a depth comparison, so small coordinate mistakes show up immediately.

Artifact notebook

The three shadow bugs beginners should learn to recognize quickly

Shadow acne

The surface shadows itself because the comparison thinks the current point is slightly behind the stored depth due to precision error.

Peter-panning

The shadow appears detached from the object because the bias is so large that the comparison starts missing valid blockers.

Blocky aliasing

The shadow edge looks chunky because the shadow map does not have enough resolution for the projected area.

Fix strategy

Simplify first: inspect light-space coordinates, visualize the shadow map, then tune bias and resolution one variable at a time.

Debug question

Does the artifact come from the light pass, the camera pass, the coordinate conversion, or the depth comparison threshold?

What comes next

Common mistake
